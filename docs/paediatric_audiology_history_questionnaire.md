# Paediatric Audiology History-Taking Questionnaire

*For use as a clinical reference and simulator scaffold. All questions are directed to the caregiver unless otherwise noted.*

---

## 1. Presenting Concern

- Why has your child been referred / what brings you in today?
- Do you personally think your child has a hearing problem?
- When did you first notice a concern, and has it changed over time?
- Is the difficulty constant or does it come and go?
- Does it seem to affect one ear more than the other, or both equally?
- Has your child had any previous hearing tests? If so, when, where, and what were the results?

---

## 2. Prenatal and Perinatal History

- How many weeks was your child when they were born?
- What was their birth weight?
- Were there any complications during the pregnancy — infections, illness, or medications?
  - *Specifically ask about: CMV, rubella, toxoplasmosis, herpes, syphilis*
- Were there any problems at birth — breathing difficulties, low oxygen, or a low Apgar score?
- Was your child admitted to the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU)?
  - If yes: how long, and was your child on a ventilator or given any antibiotics?
- Did your child have jaundice that required treatment (phototherapy or a blood transfusion)?
- Were there any visible differences in the shape of the ears, head, or face at birth?

---

## 3. Medical History

- Has your child ever had meningitis or a severe brain infection? If so, at what age?
- Has your child had frequent ear infections or been told they have fluid in the ears ("glue ear")?
  - If yes: how often, how long do episodes last, and what treatment have they had?
- Has your child had any ear surgery (grommets, mastoid surgery, or other)?
- Has your child had any significant head injury?
- Has your child ever received chemotherapy, strong antibiotics such as gentamicin, or other medications that can affect hearing?
- Does your child have any diagnosed syndrome, chromosomal condition, or neurological condition?
  - *Examples to prompt if needed: Down syndrome, CHARGE, Waardenburg, Usher, Pendred, Alport*
- Does your child have a seizure disorder?
- What medications is your child currently taking?
- Which doctors or specialists is your child currently seeing?

---

## 4. Ear and Hearing Screening History

- Was your child's hearing tested at birth (newborn hearing screening)?
  - If yes: do you know the result — pass or refer — and which ear(s)?
  - Do you know what type of test was used?
- Did your child have the B4 School Check? What was the hearing result?
- Has your child had any other hearing checks at school or at a Well Child visit?
- Is your child currently using any hearing devices — hearing aids, a bone-anchored device, or a cochlear implant?
- Does your child currently have any ear symptoms — pain, discharge, a blocked feeling, ringing, or dizziness?

---

## 5. Speech, Language, and Communication Development

*Ask about the age the child reached each milestone — prompting by approximate age if the caregiver is unsure.*

- When did your child start babbling (making repetitive sounds like "ba ba" or "da da")?
- When did they say their first real word?
- When did they start putting two words together (e.g. "more milk" or "daddy gone")?
- How many words does your child use now, approximately?
- How long are your child's sentences?
- How clearly does your child speak?
  - Can you understand them? What about close family? What about people who don't know them well?
- Do you feel your child understands more than they can say, or do both seem equally delayed?
- What language(s) are spoken at home?
- Has your child seen a speech-language therapist? If so, what did they say?

---

## 6. General Developmental History

- Did your child reach their motor milestones around the expected time — sitting, standing, walking?
- How is your child's general development — thinking, problem-solving, play?
- How does your child interact socially — with family, with other children their age?
- Has your child received any early intervention services?
- If school-aged or attending preschool: how are they getting on? Are teachers raising any concerns?
- Has your child received any formal developmental diagnosis — for example, autism, ADHD, or an intellectual disability?

---

## 7. Family History

- Is there any family history of hearing loss in childhood?
  - If yes: which relative, what type of hearing loss, at what age did it start, and what is known about the cause?
- Is there any family history of syndromes associated with hearing loss?
- Have any family members had genetic testing related to hearing?
- Are the child's parents related to each other (consanguinity)?

---

## 8. Functional Impact and Environment

- How does the hearing concern affect your child at home?
  - Do they turn the TV up? Miss things said from another room? Respond better when you are face-to-face?
- How does it affect them at preschool or school?
  - Do teachers raise concerns about listening or following instructions in the classroom?
- How does it affect their social life — playing with other children, in noisy group settings?
- Have you noticed your child being tired or irritable after a school day in a way that might relate to listening effort?
- Has your child been exposed to any loud noise — recreational, household, or otherwise?
- Is any support currently in place at school (e.g. a resource teacher, FM system, or learning support referral)?

---

## 9. JCIH 2019 Risk Factor Checklist

*Complete as a summary after taking the history above. Children with one or more risk factors warrant audiological monitoring every 6–12 months, regardless of screening outcome.*

| Risk factor | Present? |
|---|---|
| Caregiver concern about hearing, speech, language, or development | |
| Family history of permanent childhood hearing loss | |
| NICU admission >5 days, or any of: ECMO, ototoxic medications, loop diuretics, hyperbilirubinaemia requiring exchange transfusion | |
| In utero infection (CMV, herpes, rubella, syphilis, toxoplasmosis) | |
| Craniofacial anomalies including abnormal ear morphology | |
| Physical findings associated with a hearing loss syndrome | |
| Neurodegenerative disorder or sensory motor neuropathy | |
| Culture-positive postnatal infection associated with hearing loss (bacterial/viral meningitis) | |
| Head trauma, especially basal skull or temporal bone fracture | |
| Chemotherapy or radiation to head or neck | |

*Source: Joint Committee on Infant Hearing (JCIH) Year 2019 Position Statement.*

## Developmental Communication Milestones — Quick Reference

| Age | Expected communication behaviour |
|---|---|
| 0–3 months | Startles to sudden sounds; quiets or smiles in response to voice |
| 4–6 months | Turns eyes or head toward sound; babbles; laughs |
| 7–9 months | Turns directly toward a voice; responds to own name; uses non-crying vocalisations to get attention |
| 10–12 months | Understands simple commands with gesture ("come here"); first words emerging |
| 18 months | ~20 words; beginning to combine words; points to familiar objects |
| 24 months | ~50+ words; two-word phrases; strangers understand about 50% of speech |
| 3 years | Short sentences; asks simple questions; strangers understand about 75% |
| 4–5 years | Complex sentences; tells stories; almost fully intelligible to strangers |

*Note: bilingual/multilingual children may distribute vocabulary across languages — total combined vocabulary is the clinically relevant measure.*
